# Critical Reasoning

Rigorously challenge ideas, strategies, and plans to surface hidden flaws, unconsidered risks, and weak reasoning before they become expensive mistakes.

## When This Skill Applies

- Evaluating a strategy, plan, or decision before committing
- Stress-testing a hypothesis or narrative
- Preparing for objections (investor, customer, board)
- Identifying what could go catastrophically wrong
- Checking whether logic holds up under scrutiny
- Asking hard "why not" questions about a path forward

## The Core Lenses

Run through all six core lenses. Do not skip any because the idea seems solid. Then apply the conditional lens where relevant.

---

### Lens 1: Claim Validation

Identify every core claim in the idea and test each one:

- **What is being asserted?** State it plainly — strip out hedges and weasel words.
- **What evidence supports it?** Is it primary data, secondary research, analogy, or assertion?
- **What would falsify it?** If no answer exists, the claim is unfalsifiable — flag this.
- **Is the evidence actually causal or merely correlated?** Reverse the causal arrow: does it still hold?
- **What is the base rate?** How often do similar plans/ideas succeed?
- **Are there selection effects?** Are you seeing survivorship bias? Confirmation bias?

---

### Lens 2: Assumption Pressure Test

Uncover the hidden load-bearing assumptions:

- **List every assumption the plan depends on** — including the ones nobody says out loud.
- **Rank by fragility**: which assumptions, if wrong, collapse the entire plan vs. just a piece of it?
- **For each critical assumption**: What is the actual evidence that it's true? What would you need to see to believe it's false?
- **Invert each assumption**: What happens to the plan if the opposite is true?
- **Time-sensitivity**: Which assumptions are durable? Which expire in 6, 12, 24 months?

---

### Lens 3: Second-Order Impacts

Think beyond the direct effect:

- **First order**: What is the immediate, obvious consequence of this action?
- **Second order**: What does that first-order consequence cause? Who does it affect? What does it change?
- **Third order** (if material): What does the second-order effect trigger?
- **Stakeholder map**: For each party affected (customers, partners, competitors, regulators, employees), what is their likely response? Does their response create a new problem?
- **Feedback loops**: Does this action trigger a loop (virtuous or vicious)? Where does the loop stabilise — or does it?
- **Unintended consequences**: Who is harmed or disadvantaged by the plan succeeding? Do they have the ability or incentive to fight back?

---

### Lens 4: Black Swan & Tail Risk

Surface the scenarios that are low probability but high consequence:

- **What would have to be true for this to catastrophically fail?** (Not just underperform — catastrophically fail.)
- **External shocks**: What macro, regulatory, technology, or competitive events would instantly invalidate this plan?
- **Dependency failures**: What single points of failure exist? What if your key partner, supplier, or channel disappears?
- **Narrative collapse**: What is the story the market/customer/investor currently believes that this plan depends on — and what would shatter that narrative?
- **Speed asymmetry**: Are there risks that move faster than your ability to respond? (e.g., a competitor ships the same thing in 8 weeks; a regulator bans the category)
- **Known unknowns vs unknown unknowns**: List both. Acknowledge what you genuinely cannot see.

---

### Lens 5: "Why Not" Objections

Steelman every reason not to do this:

- **Why hasn't someone already done this?** If the opportunity is obvious, what has prevented it so far? Is that barrier now gone — and are you certain?
- **Why would a smart, well-resourced competitor choose NOT to enter this space?** What do they know that you don't?
- **Why might the customer say no?** List every plausible objection: cost, switching friction, trust, timing, internal politics, competing priorities.
- **Why might this be a bad idea even if it works?** (e.g., creates a market that attracts a dominant incumbent; destroys the positioning you've built elsewhere)
- **Why might the timing be wrong?** Too early (market not ready)? Too late (window closed)?
- **The "brilliant friend" test**: If a trusted, blunt advisor saw this plan, what would they say privately that they wouldn't say in the room?

---

### Lens 6: Practical Implications

Test whether the plan can actually be executed:

- **Capability gap**: What does executing this require that the team does not currently have? (Skills, relationships, data, capital, time)
- **Resource reality**: Is the plan costed honestly? What gets cut or compromised if resources are 20% lower than assumed?
- **Sequencing risk**: Are there dependencies that must happen in a specific order? What happens if one step slips?
- **Reversibility**: How easy is it to change course if early signals are negative? What are the exit costs?
- **Metric trap**: Are the success metrics measuring what actually matters, or what is easy to measure? Will hitting the metric produce the outcome — or just the appearance of it?
- **Execution vs. strategy gap**: Is this a good idea being executed by the wrong team, in the wrong market, at the wrong time?

---

## Conditional Lenses

Apply these when the subject matter matches. If the lens doesn't apply, skip it — but explicitly state that you skipped it and why.

### Lens 7: Leader First Principles

**Apply when**: Evaluating a market entry strategy, product hypothesis, or venture-scale bet. Particularly useful when the plan depends on a novel wedge, a timing advantage, or a claim of differentiation. Do not apply to purely operational or tactical decisions.

Distil the core strategic reasoning frameworks from four playbooks known for outsized outcomes. Do not invoke their personalities or attitudes — apply only the structural principles. Use each as a genuine stress-test: where does the plan hold, where does it crack?

#### 7a. Bezos / Amazon — Customer backwards + flywheel + durability

- *Work backwards from the customer*: What is the customer's actual desired outcome? Is the plan built from that outcome backwards, or from available capability forwards? If you wrote a press release announcing this was live and working perfectly — would the customer actually care?
- *What won't change in 10 years?*: Is this anchored on a durable customer need, or on a current friction caused by today's constraints (which could shift)?
- *Flywheel test*: Is there a self-reinforcing cycle? Does each customer make the product better for the next? If not, what would need to be true for a flywheel to exist?
- *Resist proxies*: Are success metrics measuring what the customer actually cares about, or convenient proxies that feel like progress but aren't?

#### 7b. Hastings / Netflix — Platform disruption + self-cannibalisation + talent leverage

- *Disrupt yourself before someone else does*: Is there a version of this that cannibalises the current plan but is a better business? If you weren't already committed to the current approach, would you still choose it?
- *Bet on the long arc, not today's constraints*: Are you over-fitting to current environmental constraints? What does this look like if those constraints relax in 3 years?
- *Talent density over process*: Does this make a great practitioner 10x more effective, or does it let an average team manage a process?

#### 7c. Nadella / Microsoft — Empathy for unmet needs + ecosystem thinking + growth mindset

- *Unmet vs unarticulated needs*: What is the job-to-be-done beneath the stated problem — the one that, once solved, makes them say "I didn't know I needed this until I had it"?
- *Ecosystem over zero-sum*: Does this sit alongside the customer's existing stack and make it more valuable, or does it require displacing something they already pay for?
- *Growth mindset signal*: Are contradictory signals being treated as data that sharpens the plan, or rationalised away?

#### 7d. Musk / SpaceX/Tesla — First principles decomposition + 10x framing + speed + deletion

- *First principles decomposition*: Strip the plan to its irreducible value exchange. What does the customer give (money, data, attention, workflow change) and what do they get? Is the exchange ratio compelling at fundamentals?
- *10x not 10%*: Is this a 10x improvement on what the customer can do today, or a marginal improvement dressed as transformation?
- *Delete before you optimise*: What requirements are being carried forward from convention rather than necessity?
- *Speed asymmetry*: Is the plan moving faster than competitors can respond? Is the time advantage structural or just temporal?

For each principle that reveals a genuine tension or gap, flag it explicitly. Do not use this lens to generate generic validation or generic concern — only surface findings that would change a decision.

---

## Synthesis: The Critical Verdict

After running all applicable lenses, produce:

1. **Validity rating**: Strong / Conditionally valid / Weak — with a one-sentence rationale
2. **Load-bearing assumptions**: The 2–3 assumptions that, if wrong, kill the plan
3. **Top risks**: Ranked by (probability x consequence), with mitigation or monitoring approach for each
4. **Strongest objection**: The single "why not" that deserves the most honest answer
5. **Leader First Principles findings** (if Lens 7 was applied): The 1–2 principles that most sharply challenged the plan, and what would need to change in response
6. **Recommended actions**: What should change, be tested first, or be explicitly de-risked before committing?
7. **Confidence statement**: What would move you from current confidence to high confidence? What evidence are you missing?

---

## Output Format

Do not produce a separate analysis document or structured report. Instead, directly update the existing document, research, or line of thinking being examined — editing claims, softening overconfident assertions, adding caveats where assumptions are fragile, inserting risks inline where relevant, and strengthening the parts that hold up. The goal is a document that has survived scrutiny, not a critique sitting beside it.

Ask the user for: the idea, plan, strategy, or decision to be stress-tested, and any specific concerns they already have that should be examined first.
